Shards & Planes

Explaining the Shards

Sixteen shards govern the entire multiverse. As their name suggests they are fragments, pieces of a whole, pieces of a God who once was, who predated the multiverse these adventures take place in - a God who was killed. Each shard is referred to by an ideal that represents the Shard's primary purpose or motivation. They are the final sources and custodians of all energy, all investiture, all magic, even of all space and time itself. Yet, to function properly, a shard needs a mind to control it, to direct its actions. A mind belonging to a sentient being who 'holds' the shard. Such a person who has taken up the power of a Shard is known as the Vessel of that Shard.
Shards typically choose their vessels, but regardless the intent and nature of the shard eventually become the intent and nature of the vessel, though individuals with strong wills can retain their own personality for long, yet even the strongest of wills would eventually find their ideals being twisted to match the shard they hold.

The Shards are the most powerful known entities in the multiverse, and for all intents and purposes, serve as its Gods. Most powers that mortals and immortals wield alike come from them. Yet some entities, aberrations of unknown origin, noticeably do not fall under any of their domains and are not allied with any of them, typically. Each shard has a outer plane of their own, though are better linked than others - this generally depends on how well the relations between the vessels are. For example, Leras and Ati were very close before becoming shards, and so it is possible to go directly from the Gardens of Preservation to the Castle of Ruin (but not the other way round) without using perpendicularities. Some shards are even said to share planes. Outer planes typically get moulded following the nature of the Shard. When creatures of the inner planes die, the Shards may choose to summon their souls to their own planes, typically this doesn't involve active action on part of the vessel.

Despite all this, the Shards themselves are almost unknown in the inner planes, and completely unknown in Materia, the material plane that forms the center of the multiverse. Instead, the pantheon that people worship are often aspects of the shard Devotion, who is - behind the veils - the final source of power for 'Gods' and their clerics alike. Similarly, other shards also grant powers to creatures - powers far beyond commoners, powers that give them supernatural abilities. Some shards' powers typically cause their recipients to go down a certain course - like Honor's powers generally give rise to Paladins (aka. Knights Radiant), while Ruin's powers generally produce the greatest Rogues.

The Nature of Investiture

Shardic powers manifest in the realms as investiture. While it may occur in all physical, cognitive and spiritual states, the greatest amount of it is present in its spiritual form. Highly 'invested' people are kept alive by the investiture that has become a part of their souls - becoming one of the reasons why a high enough level barbarian could survive a fall from outer space while a commoner would die if shoved from the fifth floor - even if they had the same hulking body with the same physical anatomy / constitution. Investiture also fuels all the abilities that creatures in the multiverse use - the ki of monks, the spell slots of wizards, etc. all are representations of how much investiture they hold presently.

That said some investiture also takes physical forms - like Preservation's mists, Ruin's Atium or Honor's 'Stormlight' - such investiture can be absorbed by creatures whose souls are invested enough to hold it and used to 'recharge' their abilities or grant them new ones. However, investiture of this form is also generally tied to the shard, and so can only be absorbed by beings with a connection to them. For example, only those bonded with spren can absorb Stormlight, or only those not touched by Ruin (i.e. those not pierced by metal) and gifted by Preservation can benefit from the mists.

Like the shards, the concepts of investiture is almost unknown in Materia, even if they do make use of it in rather considerable amounts.

Strangely, a metal yet undiscovered in the Material plane - Aluminium - repels investiture in its pure state. If some investiture is forced into it anyway (the process being a closely guarded secret), then enchanted aluminum becomes a complete void to investiture in all forms.

Honor's Sacrifice

Shards are near-infinite in their command over all physical, mental and spiritual. They can move planets on a whim, their minds are infinitely expanded and can compute the probabilities of various potential routes the future upto centuries may take with near-absolute certainly and they are the owners of all the power that circulate in the multiverse. As mentioned above, for all intents and purposes, each of them is 'God'. Yet they stand with conflicting ideals and natures - and so all conflict between mortals, or even immortals, would fade before or be subsumed under a conflict of shards. (Shardic calculations of the future, which approach near vision-level of the future gets greatly obfuscated when another being who can 'see' the future - typically another shard - is involved.)
And needless to say, a conflict between shard could've been, and once was near devastating for mortals. Plus given that shards were initially all exactly equal in strength, such a conflict would be seen through to brutal ends, and to an inconclusive result.

So like Preservation had long ago given a piece of himself to form sentient life - becoming weaker than Ruin in the process - Honor too made all the shards subject to a pact, a bond, a deal they couldn't refuse (and most didn't even want to) at the cost of making himself weaker than other shards. A pact which made them unable to use their powers to overwhelm a sentient creature from within the multiverse or use their infinite intelligence to trick or outwit it until the creature itself willed so - with full awareness of what they asked for. And so, shards were restricted to achieve their ends through sentient, often mortal, beings - people whom they would grant help and power in exchange for having them become their agents and subject to their authority.

This pact was made by invoking powers older than, and stronger than, even Shards themselves - and should they violate it, they would immediately become far weaker, and at the mercy of other shards. The same went for all future deals between sentient beings and shards. There would be no tricking or wordplay involved in such deals - both sides would have full awareness of what the other side asked for and intended. Trying to break out of such an oath would weaken the shard by a fair magnitude which depended upon the implications of breaking it. If the opposing side were to willingly break the oath, they would instantly be obliterated - erased from existence itself.

Despite that, such agreements, while not common-place, aren't exactly uncommon either. Even after knowing all implications, many mortals find that agreeing is a wiser strategy/tactic than staying away.

Of course, things from without are not subject to such restrictions...

History

Note

Time flows differently in all realms - the more the shardic focus (i.e. the greater the investiture) in a realm / point in space, the faster time moves there. For example time has not moved at all in the outer fringes of the Gardens of Preservation, where no shard focuses. So a day there would be equivalent to a century in Materia.

At first, time moved slowest in Materia - since the shards all focused on their realms. But as threats from without started to take shape in Materia, and because there was a clash of interests which unfolded there, shards shifted their focus completely over there. This reached a climax during the Silent Age, where time passed in there nearly a dozen times as fast as in the other planes. After that, during the archaic age the shards agreed to step away - causing time in the inner planes to drop till it became around half the pace of the mean of the outer planes.

However, in the last few centuries, Preservation was weakening, again causing time to go slower in the Gardens. One day there would roughly be a week in Materia. When the Survivor took up the Shard however, he immediately focused on clearing the mess in his domain, which caused time to accelerate rapidly in there causing hours spent in Ruin equivalent to weeks in the Gardens.

House Rules

In game rules

Disclaimer: These rules are based on what I felt should be appropriate based on previous experience. While I will generally stick to them, I may occasionally change them based on how the gameplay feels. Most of these are addons and interpretations, and should change anything to fundamental to the system. These take the last spot because they are entirely system dependant - and not really of much significance to the campaign itself. I may change the system, but the characters/shards would remain, while these all would become obsolete. To me the system is secondary to the narrative and world building. As such I am also typically chill with homebrew as long as I feel it won't reduce the fun other people, including myself, are having.
Of course, I will continue to add stuff here as we go along.

AoE attacks with a hit roll

Most of the times attacks targeting multiple creatures require saving throws, which typically makes sense. However, in rare cases, there may be attacks which I feel can cover multiple creatures in their impact, yet would be resisted by a sturdy piece of plate armor. Simple examples being the hail of machine gun fire or a sweeping blade of a giant. In such case I'll make a single attack roll which would apply to the primary target(s) and an amount (typically 0, 2 or 5) subtracted from the roll would apply to secondary targets in range.

Continuous Damage

If an effect deals continuous damage, it is approximated dealing D1 damage in initiative counts 24, 16 and 8 and D2 at initiative count 0, where,

  • D1 is total damage / 4, rounded down.
  • D2 is total damage - 3 × D1.

Hero Points

As the protagonists of the game there might be certain points where you might not want to leave things to rng and instead reinforce yourselves in heroic moments. So from now on, I'll be giving each pc a certain amount of hero points - an amount dependent on the length of the campaign and how major a role I want your PCs to play.

  • You can use any number of dice to add or subtract to a roll involving your character in any way.
  • The first dice is worth as many hero points you started with in total. All subsequent dice are worth one less than the previous.
  • So, if you had, say, 7 hero points. You could use them all at once to get a total of ± 28 in a single roll, probably upsetting the will of a God, or use them to get ±7 in 7 different rolls, or anything in between.
  • These will not replenish in any way whatsoever.
  • You can use these in any rolls whatsoever. (Luck rolls will be d100s in the future)
  • These add to the NAT roll, and may potentially exceed the max of the dice. And so, for combat purposes, any NAT roll ≥ 20 is considered a critical hit in to-hit rolls, and a NAT roll ≤ 1 is an auto-miss.

Note that unlike the DMG variant, these do not replenish on level ups. But they grant a much more powerful bonus to any roll, and stacking is an option. So use them wisely.

Graded rolls

Unlike the others, this is not entirely homebrew. At times we want effects from a powerful creature where we can't have the DC as per the usual formula (not much fun saying you die if you fail a DC 27 save...). So like the medusa, several high CR monsters' attacks with ridiculous DCs would have only minor effects if you fail the save by a small amount, and increasingly traumatic effects if you fail by more than a certain amount.

Honor, Sanity, Wis, Cha

Personally, I always found myself confused by when to use Wis vs Cha saving throws. So I decided to use the DMG's Honor and Sanity scores to resolve this confusion. Again, any rules I say here are my interpretation and may appear ridiculous to others, so I'll include the official words to try to reason out my homebrew distinction.

Official Rules (DMG)
  • Wis Saves. "Resisting effects that charm, frighten, or otherwise assault your willpower".
  • Cha Saves. "Withstanding effects, such as possession, that would subsume your personality or hurl you to another plane of existence".

Now to me, "assault your willpower" and "subsume your personality" sound way too close / ambiguous in certain contexts. Willpower I interpret as a measure of how much you can make yourself do something you must do even when your mind rebels against you. Will, the same, but towards a specific goal. Lacking them means you will waver in your intent (a crucial, perhaps the most crucial, part of your personality) when put under pressure.

To add to the confusion, Paladins, a class that literally derives its power from oaths, use Charisma. Oaths to me are the ultimate expression of willpower. To declare words/ideals which you will always follow no matter how much your mind, your instincts, even your very soul would crave otherwise. Also, quoting from the Stormlight Archive,

"There are no foolish oaths. All are the mark of men and true spren over beasts and subspren. The mark of intelligence, free will, and choice."

Final Rules
  • Honor. (aka Willpower) A skill whose base value equals your Charisma Save. This score would be used to force yourself to do something when your body or mind rebels against it. While this would be called as a saving throw most of the time, players can also request a roll of their own violation to resist, in a reduced way, the symptoms of effects like fear etc. Players may declare a will, something they really, really want from their very soul. On Honor rolls aligning with their will, players will have advantage, on those going against it, they'll have disadvantage.
    Note that the more willpower is used, the greater strain it puts on the mind. And characters might make themselves depressed/tired on overuse, particularly on failed rolls, whose mechanical effects I would decide when needed.
    However, successful rolls, particularly in situations that matter, might also eventually grant players an increase to the modifier itself.
  • Sanity. A skill whose base value equals your Wisdom save. This score would be used only in save-like situations, though often out of combat. It would denote how much you can hold on to your mind when exposed to the utterly alien/aberrant/powerful, such as the focus of an elder (or worse) nightmare, reading the more infernal plays of The Playwright, or encountering a more potent vision of a Shard than the mind can handle.
    Failed rolls will generally incur forms of madness or other insanity, but in more severe cases can lead to more drastic effects. For example, being exposed to the full form of an outsider might cause a feeblemind while being exposed to the full form of a shard, if it were possible, would be instant death.
    The modifier to this roll also can increase, it increases the more a character is exposed to, in a 'vaccine-like' manner. It may also decrease when exposed in a harsher way, and greatly decrease if the save made to resist it fails.
  • Saves. So what do I use the saves for? Firstly and majorly, in combat. Occasionally, I may use them for the effects listed in DMG instead of honor/sanity. Wisdom saves will be used to keep your cool, like when being charmed (which might include being forced into wrath/bloodlust). Like in the case of partly resisting fear, effects of a failed wisdom save could typically be reduced or even nullified by a successful subsequent Honor roll, but that would put some strain on the mind. Charisma saves will be made to resist personality changes on a core level - like an alignment switch. A rule of thumb will be that wisdom saves would target the immediate, while charisma saves would target the long term.

Rests and Natural healing

The reason heroes can fall from 150ft in the air, get slashed on the gut with a longsword and then be up and about after a good night's sleep without any healing magic is because of the investiture their bodies hold, which allows them to take damage that normally would kill a person. This means that the replenishment of their investiture overnight is what natural healing entails. This also means that in areas of reduced investiture, natural healing would suffer. Normally, this doesn't affect the game and we can follow the basic rules just as usual (since all campaigns happen in the outer planes with a good amount of investiture), but in areas of low, high investiture we'd adopt different rule-sets.

  • Investiture Drained areas. (Materia)
    • Any use of hit dice in short/long rests requires access to a healer's kit or someone with Medicine or nature proficiency.
    • The character doesn't auto-restore full health on a long rest, instead they restore hit dice equal to their class levels / 4, rounded down for each class they have. They must restore at least 1 hit die.
  • Investiture Void areas. (Areas with high pure Aluminium like the Plane of Innovation)
    • Gritty Realism. Short rests become 8 hours, and long rests become a week.
    • Hit dice do not contribute to max HP which instead becomes 9 + Con Mod. (Or 8 + Con Mod for small size.)
    • Characters restore 1 hp in short rests. This becomes 1d4 if someone has access to a healer's kit or has Medicine proficiency.
    • Characters restore 2 + Con Mod HP in long rests. This becomes 2d4 + Con Mod if someone has access to a healer's kit.
  • Heightened Investiture areas. (Perpendicularities, areas closer to shards / the spiritual realm)
    • Epic Heroism. Short rests become 5 minutes, long rests become 1 hour.
    • Healing surges. Characters can use a bonus action to expend 1 hit die for healing.
    • Characters restore hit dice equal to class levels / 4 on a short rest, rules same as above.

On Insight checks

Here its probably better if I start with the inspiration for this. So in my experience of the first arc of the first campaign, players would "insight check this" everytime an npc said something, or even appeared to be, remotely suspicious. Now the npcs of the first arc were generally good, but when we were about to head to the second - where betrayal, manipulation and deceit would be a thing - I didn't want these insight checks to completely trivialize any possible plot, specially when three players together called them, at least one would end up getting more ≥ 20/25.

So, to make sure insight remains close to what it is in real life, just one tool among many to sort out intrigue and plots, I came up with a simple house rule, the players wouldn't know anymore than their characters did - or specifically, they wouldn't know if the insight roll was a NAT 20 or a NAT 1. However, should their characters be confused, that's the result the players would be told. This works very well with a roll ≤ 5 being something to be afraid of, since a ≤ 5 won't just mean the roll was wasted but actually gave the players a wrong clue instead. I like this approach because it forces players to consider / look for clues other than just looking at a guys face, insight checking without any risk, and potentially ending the mystery then-and-there with an ~30 roll.

And so I went with this -

  • If a person is speaking normally / the truth, but the players are paranoid, a secret insight roll would be made, and compared against a value between 12-16 (typically 13). If it is very close to that, the character (and the player) would receive mixed signals, if it is higher, the PC would know they are genuine. If it is lower, the PC would feel they are lying instead. The further away from 13 it gets, the more confident the PC is in their assessment.
    13 was chosen as it is the expected value of an insight roll assuming 13 WIS and a half-proficiency bonus of 1, which is where I'd place a mature commoner adult. But an adventurer specializing in insight could just look at a face and recognize a genuine person when they see one, however they could be mistaken against pro liars...
  • If a person is deliberately lying, and the players are paranoid, a secret insight roll would be made against the liar's 'passive deception', which could well mean DC's of ≥ 25 since +17 to a trait is easily obtainable for rouges/bards of lv 17 and above (or experienced/magically enhanced rouges/bards of lv 13+ or even lv 10+), and advantage gives a +5 to the passive. If their insight roll is near the DC, they'll be getting mixed signals, if it's greater then, they'd detect an ulterior motive or even outright lies. If it is less than the DC, they'll believe the lie.

I feel this is balanced since a professional liar could probably not be detected that easily, else they'd have fallen long ago. But it's not all hopeless, as even the most cunning person would perhaps leave behind some clues revealing their nefarious intentions...

Escalated Damage Types

This is one of my attempts to make each damage type more distinct. In my experience, D&D damage types lack identity. Just resistances and vulnerabilities are not enough. After they are considered, there's virtually no incentive to explore or specialize in different damage types. Specially given the ease of availability of one of force, radiant and magical weapon damage for almost each class, characters have little reason to pick any other type.

So I came up with the concept of escalated damage types. These are very rare, and only found against deadly enemies or enemies from very dangerous environments. At their core they deal double damage and apply a negative effect, but the way they do that makes them particularly unique and deadly.

  1. If a target is immune to the base damage type, they may or may not be immune to the escalated damage type. For instance a construct without a brain that is immune to psychic damage is also immune to neural damage. But a demon form the abyss may take hellfire damage even if they are immune to fire damage. Resistance means nothing against escalated damage types while vulnerabilities extend.
  2. Base damage is always applied as normal first. So if an attack deals 27 neural damage, the affected target would immediately take 27 psychic damage. This is affected by vulnerability / resistance. Let's say the target was resistant, so they would 13 psychic damage now.
  3. Concentration-like checks are then made against total escalated damage since the last failed check. Such checks must only be made when the total damage exceeds 20, but the target can willingly choose to fail a save whenever they wish to. In the above example, the total damage is 27, so a DC 14 Int saving throw is attempted.
  4. If a concentration-like check is successful, nothing happens, but the damage is tracked. So in the above example let's say the target succeeds the save, then takes another 25 neural damage. On the second instance, they must make a DC 26 Int save (since the total damage is now 52).
  5. Should they fail, the total damage stacked up is immediately applied and so is an adverse effect. In this case since let's assume the failed the save. So as a result of that attack the damage taken is 52 + 25/2 = 64. This abrupt application of damage is what makes escalated damage so deadly, since it may easily one-shot rather tanky characters too. Since the damage proc'd was neural, the target suffering the damage would also be stunned for 1 round.
  6. Let's say the victim didn't want this landfall damage, so they could've chosen to fail the save immediately when they took the first 27 psychic damage. They'd then have taken 40 psychic damage and been stunned. So the tradeoff is that while the damage taken is more gradual (giving allies a chance to heal), the negative effect becomes more frequently applied.

Some escalated damage types encountered so far are -

Meta-game rules

Note: This section of the rules aren't really to do with either the narrative or the system, but are more of implementation details I thought I should be clear upon, so that we can all have the best time possible with the resources available to us.

On optimization and Roleplay

As an optimizer myself, there's no way I'd ever frown on optimization. It's totally fine for players to optimize their builds, and they will feel rewarded for optimization by having their character excel in whatever they are optimized for. But I would prefer if players build characters with what they want to role-play as first in mind as compared to what is mechanically strong. As I've mentioned before, gameplay is secondary to narrative in my games, so I'm totally fine with homebrewing something if players (and I) feel their character is weaker because of a choice they made for roleplaying's sake. (And the best way to convince me would be to present an alternate build with similar resources as your character was built with but with better gameplay.)

The reason I mention this specifically is that I will take the rp-aspects of builds as seriously as I would take the mechanics, if not more. If someone plays a paladin, they would have to roleplay all the oaths. If someone plays a wizard, I will expect them to do some arcane research in character. Conversely, if someone plays a melee class, odds are I will not even allow them to attempt arcana checks, as their characters would have no idea of most arcane bullshit that can only be found in libraries. If you are a Cleric/Druid with 16+ Wis I would very much frown upon roleplaying an edgy character. If you are a warlock, well your patron probably owns your soul, and they would use that sometime for their personal gain - possibly in ways you, as a player, may not like.

Following with the character sheet being consistent with RP, I will allow players to 'make trades' with their character sheets. Give something up to gain something else. An example that comes to mind is that someone may be playing an introvert warlock - whose patron chose them because of their inner force of personality, force of will - not because they were good at singing or dancing. If someone wants to rp that I'll allow them to give up their charisma bonus to performance (and probably persuasion) in exchange for a bonus to Cha saves. I'm not yet fully sure how I'll implement that - but most probably any player who wants a trade will list the things they gain/loose, and I'll allocate points (positive or negative) to each separately. Then each player would be allowed to add anything from the joint pool of traits such that the sum of their points is close to zero.

Campaign monitoring

While I was relatively lazy in the previous campaign to go through the character sheets in depth - this time I will make sure to give each of them thorough scrutiny pre-session to make sure the gameplay is in sync with what's in there. And I would prefer there to the no surprises on the mechanics front. I would love to be surprised by how you use what you can do, but hate to be surprised to learn what you can do. As such I'd strongly prefer if players tell me upfront what they think they're the strongest moves and weakest points are themselves. The stuff they've optimized and the stuff they've dumped.

Like I said, I like to reward optimization, so you needn't worry that I will design enemies just to bring out the weak points of an optimized character. But I will have players commit to a build if they want to use it. One example that comes to mind is the free usage of mounts in the first campaign. I've no issues in having players with mounts that can fly, climb walls, or zoom past at 120 ft/turn speed, but giving them for free seems a disservice to the other players. This time there will be enemies who will first target the mount before they take down the character. So if you want to do a mounted gameplay, better commit resources into protecting your mount. Likewise, if you want to play a flying character, better think of a contingency plan for when you're knocked unconscious in the sky, etc.

Another thing I'll be taking much more seriously in future games is roll fudging. While being honest about their dice rolls is something I'd have liked to entrust to players (after all bad rolls offer just as good roleplaying opportunities as good ones), I personally want every Nat 20 to be a celebration, to all players as well as myself, and that cannot happen, at least for me, if I'm paranoid of whether the rolls were fudged. And so, while I allowed the use of beyond20 and irl dice in the first campaign - I'm going to crack down on it henceforth.

While I understand the motivations of those using irl dice, and know fudging is definitely not among them, I will reserve the right to not allow irl dice for any roll I deem significant. Beyond20 is still allowed, but only for rolls that are macros (like you press a button on your sheet and auto-generated stuff rolls), examples being an attack/damage roll made together. For arbitrary dice rolls - like a simple 2d6 or 1d12 + 1d8 without context, I will not accept anything outside of roll 20. Notably, luck rolls are covered in this - only rolls made within r20 are accepted. In return, I won't have to do something like making 1 the best result, or choosing 4 random numbers, ever again. Furthermore, for any roll made outside roll 20, which comes after a noticeable delay, I reserve the right to ask the player to re-roll should I wish to. I will also be going through the roll20 logs and comparing them to the D&D Beyond logs arbitrarily, and really hope I do not come across any discrepancies.

Active NPC interaction

How does this work?

  • The overall attitude score displayed is the sum of 4 components - Trust, Gratitude, Respect and Affection. The first three are indicated in the cyclic border around the numbers in the mentioned order going clockwise.
  • Ratings. Each component can take a rating between [-10, 10]. Negative values are categorized by Paranoia, Envy, Contempt and Hatred respectively.
  • Scores. Every component has its score. The Rating is a function of that score. For example, a score between (-3, 3) grants a Rating of 0, one within [3, 7) grants a Rating of 1, [7, 12) grants a Rating of 2, etc.
  • Interactions with an NPC can increase or decrease said scores on one or more axis.
  • Attitude zones. Attitude ratings between each component are divided into various zones - like {..., (-4, -1], (-1, 1), [1, 4), [4, 7) ...} etc. The cyclic border's color and opacity for the three components depends on the zone the rating is in. The zone also affects other features described below.
  • Note: The divisions between ratings is not linear. It is far easier to go from 1 respect to 2 respect than it is to go from 9 to 10. Not only is the gap longer, but the effect of favourable actions reduces while unfavourable actions increases at higher zones.
  • Forgiveness. When decent levels of say, Trust are gained, an NPC would simply ignore minor issues which would otherwise have sparked Paranoia. This is handled by having a 'forgiveness buffer' whose size depends on the current rating. The buffer is currently as much as the score needed to attain the next rating. The forgiveness buffer is depleted before actual trust value is lost, and it is replenished before too. The increased/reduced effects of actions at high ratings while affecting score do not apply to the buffer.
  • Forgetfulness. The scores of each component individually decay towards zero on a daily basis. This decay is unaffected by the forgiveness buffer and is lower at more extreme zones. The natural decay will never cross a zone boundary. (That was the inspiration for zones in the first place - once you say cross Trust Rating 7, the NPC will make a mental note regarding you as "trusted", after which just the passage of time will not cause them to forget the note.)
